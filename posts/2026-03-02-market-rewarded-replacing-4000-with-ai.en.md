# The Market Just Rewarded Replacing 4,000 People With AI

Last week, Block cut 4,000 employees after deploying an internal AI agent called “Goose.”

Code output per engineer increased by 40%.  
Revenue per employee jumped from roughly $2.2M to $4.2M.  
The stock climbed more than 15%.

Markets interpreted this as structural efficiency, not desperation.

This wasn’t a distressed company slashing costs to survive. It was a profitable company compressing its operating system - and being rewarded for it.

That’s the signal.

---

## The Part Most Leaders Are Missing

Most companies still treat labor as default infrastructure.

More tickets? Hire.  
More product velocity? Hire.  
More reporting? Hire.

Headcount becomes the throughput lever.

But labor doesn’t just add cost. It adds coordination.

More engineers means more pull requests.  
More pull requests mean more reviews.  
More reviews mean more meetings.  
More meetings mean slower shipping.

The weight compounds quietly.

---

## What Actually Changes Inside the System

A 40% jump in code output doesn’t come from motivation.

It comes from workflow compression.

Imagine this:

An engineer writes a feature spec.  
AI drafts the initial PR.  
AI generates unit tests.  
AI flags edge cases.  
AI handles first-pass code review against style and security rules.

The engineer doesn’t disappear.

They move up a layer - from typing to deciding.

One engineer now pushes through what previously required multiple back-and-forth cycles.

That’s not “AI helping.”

That’s AI replacing friction.

When friction drops, output per head rises.  
When output per head rises, revenue per head follows.

Arithmetic, not optimism.

---

## The Consequence No One Is Modeling

Public markets just rewarded headcount compression tied to AI.

That changes incentives.

Because capital markets are enforcement mechanisms.

If revenue per employee becomes the benchmark, boards will start asking different questions:

Why hasn’t output per engineer increased?  
Why are support costs rising per customer?  
Why does it take 14 people to do what another firm does with 6?

Once the benchmark resets, “normal” starts to look bloated.

And bloated gets repriced.

---

## A Mid-Quarter Thought Experiment

Picture two companies entering next year.

Company A hires 30 more people to handle growth.  
Payroll rises. Complexity rises. Reporting layers expand.

Company B redesigns workflows.  
AI handles first drafts, first responses, first reviews.  
Headcount stays flat. Output climbs.

Both grow revenue.

Only one grows revenue per employee.

In 18 months, that won’t be a brag metric. It will be table stakes.

---

## AI Has Crossed the Infrastructure Line

Jack Dorsey admitted the move was somewhat preemptive - a bet that AI has already shifted from “promising tool” to load-bearing infrastructure.

That’s the real inflection.

AI is no longer an assistant sitting beside your team.

It’s becoming the execution layer beneath them.

Human judgment.  
Machine execution.

Input -> AI process -> Human oversight.

When that loop stabilizes, the org chart changes.

Not because leadership wants to cut.

Because the system requires fewer hands to produce the same output.

---

## Two Futures

Future one:

You continue solving throughput with hiring.  
Margins tighten slowly. Coordination drag grows. Every new initiative requires more bodies to push it forward.

Future two:

You redesign around automation.  
Headcount stabilizes. Output per employee rises. The team focuses on edge cases, judgment, and leverage - not repetition.

Both paths feel rational today.

Only one compounds cleanly.

---

## The Quiet Reality

When the benchmark resets, laggards don’t get sympathy.

They get repriced.

If investors evaluated your company tomorrow on revenue per employee, would the number look intentional - or accidental?

Because the market just made something clear:

AI efficiency is no longer experimental.

It’s starting to define who looks heavy - and who looks inevitable.
